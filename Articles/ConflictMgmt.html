Approaches to Conflict and the Way of Christ

Understanding how different philosophies handle conflict—and why Scripture calls us to something deeper.

Conflict Management Devotional More Articles

Published: January 27, 2026
Author: Brent D Kern

Conflict is an unavoidable reality in a fallen world. Wherever people live, work, worship, or serve together, differences emerge. Scripture never promises a conflict-free life. Instead, it reveals that conflict often becomes the place where hearts are exposed and spiritual maturity is tested.

How we approach conflict reveals what we believe about power, truth, love, and ultimately God Himself. Over time, cultures, organizations, and churches have adopted various philosophies to manage conflict. Some reduce tension temporarily, others achieve quick resolution—but few bring lasting transformation.

Why Conflict Matters

From the opening chapters of Genesis, conflict emerges as a result of sin. Broken relationships—with God and with one another—become the norm. Yet throughout Scripture, God continually moves toward reconciliation. Conflict, when handled well, becomes a sacred space where repentance, forgiveness, and restoration can take root.

Common Philosophies of Conflict Management

Avoidance

Avoidance treats conflict as inherently dangerous. Silence, withdrawal, and disengagement are used to keep the peace. While this may prevent immediate escalation, unresolved conflict often resurfaces with greater intensity. Avoidance protects comfort, but rarely produces healing.

Accommodation

Accommodation values harmony above all else. One party yields in order to preserve peace. Though sometimes appropriate, chronic accommodation erodes integrity and fosters resentment. Peace achieved through self-erasure is fragile and unsustainable.

Competition (Power-Based)

Competition views conflict as a contest to be won. Authority, force, or influence determine outcomes. While decisive and occasionally necessary in crisis situations, this approach damages trust and produces compliance without commitment. Fear replaces unity.

Compromise

Compromise seeks fairness by mutual concession. Each party gives something up in order to move forward. Though practical, compromise often avoids deeper heart issues and leaves no one truly satisfied. Transformation requires more than mutual loss.

Collaboration

Collaboration focuses on understanding underlying needs and creating shared solutions. It encourages empathy, creativity, and partnership. However, collaboration assumes goodwill and trust. Where sin, power imbalance, or unresolved wounds exist, collaboration alone is insufficient.

The Biblical Vision of Conflict

Scripture offers a radically different vision. Conflict is not merely a problem to solve—it is an opportunity for spiritual formation. Jesus neither avoids conflict nor dominates it. He enters conflict redemptively.

Biblical peacemaking holds truth and grace together. Sin is named honestly. Responsibility is embraced personally. Forgiveness flows freely, yet wisdom establishes boundaries. Restoration is pursued whenever possible, but righteousness is never sacrificed for false peace.

At the cross, justice and mercy meet. Christ exposes sin fully while offering forgiveness completely. This cross-shaped reconciliation becomes the model for Christian conflict management.

Why Our Approach

At Christian Steps Ministries, we believe conflict handled apart from Christ becomes destructive. Our approach is rooted in Scripture, shaped by humility, and focused on restoration rather than victory.

We help individuals, leaders, and organizations move beyond surface solutions toward genuine reconciliation—forming healthier relationships, stronger leadership, and communities that reflect the character of Christ.

True peace is not the absence of conflict, but the presence of righteousness, love, and restored relationships.
