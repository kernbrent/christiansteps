Have You Ever Fed Jesus?

Jesus says that what we do for the overlooked and hurting is done to Him. That truth changes how we see people—and how we love.

A simple question can uncover a much deeper truth: Have you ever fed Jesus? Most of us would answer, “Of course not.” Jesus walked the hills of Galilee two thousand years ago. We can’t set a plate in front of Him the way the disciples once did.

And yet, Jesus Himself says otherwise. In Matthew 25, He describes the day when the Son of Man comes in glory and the nations are gathered before Him. The King welcomes some into His kingdom with a surprising reason: “I was hungry and you gave me something to eat… I was thirsty and you gave me something to drink… I was a stranger and you invited me in.” Then comes the line that lands on the heart like thunder: “Whatever you did for one of the least of these… you did for me.”

The “least of these” have faces

It’s easy to keep need at a distance. We talk about “the homeless,” “the poor,” “refugees,” “prisoners,” and “those people.” Without realizing it, we can remove their faces—turn them into categories instead of human beings. But people are not categories. People have names. People have stories. People have hopes, fears, preferences, and wounds.

Sometimes God teaches this truth in a moment so small that it feels almost ridiculous—until it isn’t. Imagine offering a sandwich and a drink to someone living on the street, only to have them refuse it because the drink is the “wrong color.” The request can sound ungrateful until the Spirit shines a light on what’s really happening: the person in front of you is not an object of charity. They are a human being—made in God’s image—who simply wants a red juice box.

That small preference becomes a holy mirror. It exposes the quiet pride that can live inside good deeds: “Be thankful for what I decided to give you.” But love doesn’t speak that way. Love sees a face. Love honors a person. Love does not reduce someone to what they lack.

Feeding Jesus is more than food

Jesus lists real needs—hunger, thirst, clothing, illness, prison—but the heart of His teaching is not merely logistics. It’s presence. It’s dignity. It’s compassion that refuses to look away. Sometimes “feeding” looks like a meal. Sometimes it looks like listening. Sometimes it looks like remembering someone’s name when the world has forgotten it.

And yes—it can feel scary. Need is messy. People are complex. Some situations are uncomfortable. But Jesus does not ask His people to love from a safe distance. He draws us into a love that crosses the street, touches a hand, and offers mercy without contempt.

Cast the net on the other side

After the resurrection, the disciples found themselves drifting back to what they knew. Peter said, “I’m going fishing,” and the others went with him. All night they caught nothing. Then a man on the shore called out, “Throw your net on the right side of the boat and you will find some.” When they obeyed, the net filled so completely they could hardly haul it in. (John 21)

The miracle is beautiful—but notice the invitation underneath it. When fear, confusion, or disappointment presses in, we often retreat to what is familiar. Jesus gently interrupts: Try again—another way. Cast the net on the other side. For some, that “other side” might be a new season, a new calling, or a new obedience. For others, it might be the courage to move toward the hurting instead of stepping around them.

The story continues on the shore, where Jesus is already cooking breakfast. He feeds the disciples. He restores Peter. And He wraps the call of discipleship into one repeated phrase: “Feed my sheep.”

Where will we find ourselves?

Matthew 25 is both warning and invitation. Jesus is not playing word games when He says, “You did it for me.” He is revealing what love looks like in His kingdom: a life that sees Him in the overlooked.

The good news is that this is not about earning God’s love. It is about becoming the kind of people who reflect God’s love. Each day, we get to choose what our lives will say about the King we claim to follow.

So start small. Start human. Learn someone’s name. Offer the sandwich. Listen for five minutes. Refuse to steal a person’s face by turning them into an “issue.” And when pride rises—when you feel the old impulse to control, judge, or dismiss—remember: sometimes a holy moment looks like a simple request… “I want a red one.”

To God be all the glory.
