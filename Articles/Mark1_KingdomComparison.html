Mark 1: The Kingdom of God Has Come Near

In Mark 1, the kingdom is not an idea—it’s God’s reign arriving in the person of Jesus, revealed through authority, mercy, and an invitation to repent and believe.

Mark 1 does more than introduce Jesus—it announces a new reality. After the baptism, the wilderness testing, and John’s imprisonment, Jesus steps forward with a proclamation that sets the tone for the entire Gospel: “The time has come… The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news!” (Mark 1:15). Mark is showing us that the King has arrived, and therefore the kingdom is breaking in.

“The time has come”: God’s clock, not ours

Jesus’ message begins with timing: “The time is fulfilled.” Mark wants the reader to hear the weight of that phrase. This is not merely “something important is happening.” It is “God’s appointed season has arrived.” The story Israel has been living—promise, longing, anticipation— is moving into fulfillment in Jesus.

What does it mean that the kingdom is “near”?

The kingdom of God is not primarily a location. It is God’s reign—His rightful rule made visible, active, and personal. In Mark 1, that reign shows up immediately in what Jesus does: He calls disciples, teaches with authority, drives out unclean spirits, heals the sick, cleanses the outcast, and confronts oppression. The kingdom is “near” because the King is near.

Authority that exposes the true King

Mark repeatedly uses the language of authority in chapter 1. Jesus teaches “not as the teachers of the law,” but with authority. Even the unclean spirits recognize it. Sickness yields to it. Isolation is undone by it. Mark is painting a portrait: the kingdom is present because Jesus carries the authority of God Himself.

Repent and believe: the doorway into the kingdom

Jesus’ invitation is not simply “admire” or “observe.” It is “repent and believe.” Repentance is not mere regret; it is a turning—a reorientation of allegiance. Belief is not mere agreement; it is trust—receiving the good news as true and letting it reshape life. Mark 1 shows that the kingdom is not forced on people; it is announced and received. The first disciples respond by leaving nets, routines, and identities to follow the King.

Good news for a world full of competing ‘lords’

Just as Rome proclaimed its emperors as saviors and lords, our world offers rival kingdoms—power, success, fear, comfort, and self. Mark’s first chapter confronts all of them gently but firmly: the true kingdom is God’s, and the true King is Jesus. The good news is not that we can climb into power, but that God has come near to rescue, heal, and reign.

Mark 1 invites us to a simple response: listen to the King, trust the good news, and step into the life of God’s reign. The kingdom is near—not because we found God, but because God came looking for us.

To God be all the glory.
