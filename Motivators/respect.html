Respect

           

Understanding Respect

People with a strong desire for respect want to be important and respected. They need others to look up to them for their achievements or their unique talents. Their admirable qualities inspire awe that can lead others to fear them.

If you have a high drive for respect, you work hard at being unique and special.

You want to lead, not follow, and you often thrive in the limelight. You crave respect and admiration, and you hold high standards with discipline. You may have a prophetic boldness that can speak truth to power. Being heard and obeyed can feel like something you should expect.

Obedience and admiration are not the same thing as love. You may sometimes prefer being right over being loved, which can distance relationships. Admiration from the crowd is fleeting, and crowds can turn when approval becomes tiring. Those who love you for who you are will offer grace and forgiveness. Scripture warns that the self-exalting will be humbled and the humble will be exalted (Matthew 23:12; Luke 14:11). God’s humbling lessons can feel harsh, but they can also be healing.

You can never fully satisfy the need to be important.

There will always be someone with more, who has done more, or who seems better at something. Cherish your place in the body of Christ, remembering Christ is the head of the church, not you (Ephesians 1:22-23; Colossians 1:18). In the church, we are one body with different roles—no part is more essential than another (1 Corinthians 12:12-27). There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit distributes them (1 Corinthians 12:4). True respect comes from faithfully using your gifts to build up the body, not from outshining others.

A drive for respect can sometimes come across as arrogance, self-serving ambition, or coldness.

Hierarchy and comparison can make it hard to relate, because differences become louder than common ground. In church life, that can lead to division when titles matter more than unity. Jesus taught that greatness in His kingdom looks like servanthood (Mark 10:43-45). Let love flow even when you do not feel important.

Because respect matters to you, you may scan the environment for signs of disrespect. You may feel ready to correct anyone who fails to notice your uniqueness. If your respect is tied to appearance or performance, you may push your body too far to stay strong, beautiful, or impressive.

You do not tolerate rejection well, and being overshadowed can feel unbearable.

You may go to extremes to save face and avoid situations where you are not admired.

As a leader, you set goals, take responsibility, work hard, and strive for excellence.

You stand up for what you believe and do not cower from confrontation. This becomes unhealthy when the goal is getting your own way or when others pay the cost. Jesus did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give His life as a ransom for many (Mark 10:45). Learn to delegate and allow others to do things their way, even if it is not perfect. To each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good (1 Corinthians 12:7). When you do a job someone else could do, you may rob them of a chance to grow.

You may enjoy describing your accomplishments or sacrifices so others notice your excellence.

When coaching, avoid turning the moment into an opportunity to tell people what to do. Instead, guide them with questions and listen well, leaving space for them to learn. Use pauses to let people respond, even when you already know the answer.

You may feel stressed when you must follow someone else or when work falls below your standards.

Resist the urge to go back and make everything perfect when it is good enough to move forward. Remember: the time it takes to perfect something is not always worth the cost.

Watch the tendency to treat feedback as criticism.

Others may be trying to help you, not diminish you. You may become irritable when people give direction, because you want to be seen as a leader, not a follower. You may be quick to anger when people disagree, or when they move slower than you would. Pause, slow down, and breathe. Trust that God is working in every situation. He loves other people too, even if you feel like you are His favorite.

Learn to value love and connection without needing respect and admiration.

Cherish humility, patience, and kindness. Love is patient and kind; it does not envy, boast, or act proud (1 Corinthians 13:4-5). It does not dishonor others, it is not self-seeking, and it keeps no record of wrongs (1 Corinthians 13:4-5). In church life, pride opposes grace: God opposes the proud but shows favor to the humble (James 4:6; 1 Peter 5:5). Write the love poem and place it where you will see it often.

Seek opportunities to blend in rather than stand out.

Be a member of the body of Christ, allowing others to shine in their unique way (Romans 12:4-5). Applaud others, doing nothing out of selfish ambition or vain conceit (Philippians 2:3). Ask others for direction, wisdom, and guidance. Try activities where you do not naturally excel, and treat them as learning opportunities. God loves you for who you are, not for what you have or what you do. Learn contentment in all things (Philippians 4:11). Live with joy!

Spiritual Reflection

  • Where am I seeking admiration or importance instead of resting in my identity in Christ?
  • How do I respond when my leadership, ideas, or efforts go unnoticed or unaffirmed?
  • In what ways might God be inviting me to practice humility, servanthood, or hidden faithfulness?
  • How can I use my gifts this season to build others up rather than to stand out or prove myself?

Respect (Summary)

Respect is a desire to be seen as important, respected, and set apart. When this drive is surrendered to Christ, it can fuel excellence, courage, and leadership that strengthens the church. Scripture reminds us that real worth comes from belonging to Christ and serving His body, not from outshining others or chasing admiration.

The growth edge is humility and love: learning servanthood, receiving feedback, delegating, and celebrating others. Instead of scanning for disrespect or protecting your image, practice contentment and patient love. As you value people over applause, your gifts become a blessing—steady, Christ-centered, and life-giving.

Respect (Overview)

Respect is the God-given desire to live with identity and purpose in Christ—using your gifts to serve, lead, and build up others rather than chasing admiration.
